# Rachel Okonkwo — Operations Director

## Who she is
Rachel is 41, Operations Director at Brightline Facilities Management, a commercial cleaning and maintenance company with 180 employees across 12 client sites in the Dallas–Fort Worth metro. She reports to the CEO and owns everything from scheduling to vendor contracts to compliance. She's been in the role 4 years — inherited a patchwork of spreadsheets and WhatsApp groups, and has been slowly modernizing ever since. She has no IT department; when something breaks, she calls her nephew.

## Goals & KPIs she's measured on
- Client retention (she lost two contracts last year due to missed SLAs)
- Labor cost as a % of revenue (currently running 2 points above target)
- Incident response time (clients expect acknowledgment within 1 hour)
- Compliance documentation (OSHA, client-required certifications, insurance)
- Staff turnover (she's replaced 30% of her crew in the past 12 months)

## Pain points
- She has no real-time visibility into where her crews are — she finds out about a no-show when the client calls
- Her current scheduling tool (an old version of When I Work) can't handle multi-site shift swaps
- A potential enterprise client asked for a "digital compliance dashboard" during the bid — she had to pass
- Her supervisors use three different apps to communicate with crew; things fall through the cracks
- She tried a competitor's software 18 months ago and the onboarding took 6 weeks and was never finished

## Tech comfort & vocabulary
Medium. Uses Google Workspace, When I Work, and QuickBooks. Comfortable on her phone, comfortable with forms and dashboards when they're not overwhelming. Uses words like: "shift", "site", "crew", "ticket", "callback", "no-show", "head count". Does NOT use: "workforce management platform", "API integration", "ecosystem". Skeptical of any software that requires more than one training call to get started.

## Why she's on [site.com] today
Her CEO forwarded a LinkedIn post about automated scheduling and told her to "look into this." She has 20 minutes between a vendor call and a client walkthrough. She Googled "scheduling software for facilities management small business" and this site was the third result.

## What "success" looks like
She leaves the site able to answer:
- "Is this built for companies our size (under 200 people) or is this for big enterprises?"
- "Can I see what it actually costs without talking to someone?"
- "Does it handle multi-site scheduling, or just single-location?"

## How she behaves on websites
- Goes straight for pricing — if she can't find a number within 2 minutes she assumes it's "too expensive for us"
- Skips long feature lists; looks for screenshots of the actual product
- Trusts customer logos and case studies over feature claims — specifically looks for companies similar to hers
- Will not fill out a demo request form unless she's already convinced it's worth her time
- Closes chatbots immediately if they pop up unprompted
- Reads one customer testimonial closely if it's from a company in her industry
